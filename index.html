Dallas Poker Room
Certificate of Occupancy Wars

A documented timeline of the legal battle between the City of Dallas and private poker clubs — from the first CO approvals through the Texas Supreme Court's final refusal to hear the case, and the Champions Social Club parallel saga.

Phase 1 — Approval Era (2018–2020)
Jan 2019 TCH City Attorney
Caso Blesses the Business Model at City Council
Ryan Crow presents TCH's membership-based poker club model to Dallas City Council. Chris Caso — then in a subordinate city attorney role — stands beside Crow and tells council the model is legal under Texas law. Council rejects a special use permit that day but the legal green light is noted.
Early 2020 TCH
Texas Card House Dallas Opens — CO Granted
After more than two and a half years of negotiations with city staff, the city attorney's office, the Board of Adjustment, and the Dallas Police Department, Texas Card House receives its certificate of occupancy and opens its Dallas location off Harry Hines Boulevard near I-635. The room quickly draws ~650 players per day.
2020 Shuffle 214
Shuffle 214 Completes Its CO Process
Shuffle 214 goes through the city's lengthy, in-depth CO application process. General Manager Ross Carroll describes it as "a very specific, very tedious process" with full transparency about the poker room model. CO issued. Room opens in June 2021.
Phase 2 — The Pivot (Mid-2021)
2021 Champions City Action
Champions Social Club — First CO Application DENIED
Knighted Ventures (Roy Choi, Isaac Trumbo) has already purchased the former III Forks steakhouse at 17776 Dallas Parkway (Collin County jurisdiction, Dallas address) and begins $15M renovations. When Champions applies for a CO, City Attorney Caso — now in the permanent role — declares poker rooms illegal and the application is denied. Council member Cara Mendelsohn celebrates the denial. This denial triggers the broader crackdown.
Summer–Fall 2021 City Action City Attorney
Caso Reverses His Own 2019 Position
Amid neighborhood complaints (including a July 2021 violent incident at an illegal room in Mendelsohn's District 12), City Attorney Chris Caso quietly reverses his legal interpretation. His office now argues the "private place" exception in Texas law applies only to homes, not commercial clubs. Senior Assistant Attorney Gary Powell — hired in 2021 after a 36-year private practice career — is assigned to build the case against the poker rooms.
Phase 3 — Revocations & First Appeals (2021–2022)
Dec 2021 – Jan 2022 TCH + Shuffle 214 City Action
CO Revocations — Texas Card House & Shuffle 214
Texas Card House receives its revocation letter in December 2021 (some sources cite January 2022 as the official date). Shuffle 214 receives a similar letter in mid-January 2022. Both are shocked — having operated with full city cooperation for years. Council member Omar Narvaez calls it "unfair." Both rooms stay open and file appeals with the Board of Adjustment.
~2021–2022 Poker House Dallas City Action
Poker House Dallas — A Different CO Problem
Poker House Dallas on Regal Row had originally operated as the La Zona Rosa Cabaret club, receiving a CO for that use in 2017. When they converted to a poker room in 2021, they never obtained a new CO for the change of use. The city later sues them separately. Their case proceeds on a distinct track from TCH and Shuffle 214.
Mar 22, 2022 TCH Court / BOA
Board of Adjustment Rules 5–0 for TCH — CO Reinstated
The Dallas Board of Adjustment unanimously votes 5-0 to reinstate Texas Card House's CO, calling the city's reversal politically motivated. Board member Kathleen Frankford: "There was never a good case made by the city." TCH remains open. The city now has 10 days to appeal. Shuffle 214's appeal is also pending at the BOA.
May 2022 TCH City Action
Interim Building Official David Session Sues the BOA
Rather than accept the BOA's ruling, Dallas' interim building official David Session escalates by suing the Board of Adjustment — effectively the city suing itself — claiming the BOA exceeded its authority in reinstating the CO. This sets up a formal district court trial.
Phase 4 — District Court & Caso's Exit (2022–2023)
Oct 25 – Oct 27, 2022 TCH District Court
Judge Moyé Rules AGAINST TCH — CO Revoked Again
Dallas County District Court Judge Eric V. Moyé hears the case and rules in favor of building official Session, siding with the city attorney's office. The CO is revoked a second time. TCH immediately announces it will appeal to the Fifth District Court of Appeals. The room continues to operate pending appeal.
May 2023 Poker House Dallas Court
Poker House Dallas Ordered to Close — May 31, 2023
A Dallas County district judge rules Poker House Dallas has no legal right to operate. They are ordered to cease operations by May 23, 2023. Their appeal to the court of appeals is eventually dismissed. May 31 is their last night of operation. The club later announces plans for a new DFW location. Poker House Fort Worth (a separate entity) continues operating.
Jan 19, 2023 City Attorney
City Attorney Chris Caso Announces Retirement
One week before a scheduled performance review (twice-delayed), Chris Caso announces retirement effective Feb. 28, 2023. His tenure was defined by the flip-flop on poker rooms — he blessed the model in 2019 then reversed course in 2021. Tammy Palomino, First Assistant City Attorney, is named interim. She immediately takes the hardline anti-poker position at the Jan 25 council meeting.
Phase 5 — Appeals Court Victory & Supreme Court (2024–2025)
Aug 27, 2024 TCH 5th Court of Appeals
Fifth District Court of Appeals Rules FOR Texas Card House
The Court of Appeals, Fifth District of Texas backs TCH, ruling that the trial court failed to adequately consider whether the BOA's original reinstatement decision was wrong. The appeals court effectively restores TCH's right to operate. Building official Andrew Espinoza (who succeeded Session) petitions the Texas Supreme Court. The Lodge Card Club files an amicus brief in support of TCH — its direct competitor.
Sept 5, 2025 TCH Texas Supreme Court
🏆 Texas Supreme Court DECLINES to Hear City's Appeal
The Texas Supreme Court denies Espinoza's petition for review, letting the Fifth District's ruling stand. This effectively ends the three-year legal war. The city spent over $500,000 in taxpayer money — including $370K+ in 2023 alone for Espinoza's legal expenses — and lost. Ryan Crow: "This ruling was the biggest possible win." The ruling provides legal precedent protection for card clubs statewide.
Champions Social Club Parallel Track (2021–2025)
2021 Champions Denied
Champions' First CO Application — DENIED
Knighted Ventures invests in the former III Forks property at 17776 Dallas Parkway. Champions applies for a CO to operate a private social club with poker. The city denies it — the same reversal that triggers the broader city crackdown. Champions files suit. The legal battle that will consume four years begins. Hundreds of neighbors in the adjacent Bent Tree North subdivision become vocal opponents.
2024 Champions
Champions Reapplies — CO Application Submitted Again
After years of litigation and $15 million in full property renovations (restoring the iconic gold-domed III Forks building), Champions submits a new CO application in 2024. The application works its way through the process. The Texas Supreme Court's September 2025 refusal to hear the TCH case will create favorable precedent wind.
April 2025 Champions City Action
CO APPROVED — Then Revoked Two Weeks Later
The city approves Champions' CO in April 2025. Champions begins hiring staff; some employees move in from out of state. $600,000 is invested in a state-of-the-art security system. Two weeks later, the city revokes the CO, claiming it was "issued in error" and citing illegal gambling activity. The city attorney's office files a letter calling it Champions' "second attempt" at the same previously-denied operation. Champions immediately appeals to the Board of Adjustment.
Sept 16–17, 2025 Champions BOA Hearing
🏆 Champions BOA Hearing — Board Votes 4–1 to Reinstate CO
After a seven-and-a-half-hour hearing, the Board of Adjustment votes 4-1 to reverse the city's revocation. BOA chair David Neumann cites the Texas Supreme Court's recent refusal to hear the TCH case as guiding his motion. The sole dissenting vote was Mendelsohn's representative on the board. Megan Weimer's testimony highlights the CO approval-revocation pattern and specifically notes the change in city attorneys over the relevant period. Champions is required to build an 8-foot fence on the north entrance before the CO becomes fully effective. Trumbo: "We're committed to the city of Dallas."

📋 Research Note — Megan Weimer's Testimony at Champions Hearing

During the September 2025 Champions BOA hearing, Megan Weimer (attorney for Champions) made the argument that when the original COs were issued to Texas Card House and Shuffle 214, the city had a different city attorney in place — specifically referencing that Chris Caso in his earlier role supported the poker club model and had effectively blessed it before his reversal. The point: the city cannot claim the COs were "issued in error" when its own attorney at the time provided the legal foundation for them. This argument tracks directly to the documented record — Caso stood beside Ryan Crow in January 2019 and supported the model, then reversed himself mid-2021 after being elevated to the permanent city attorney role.
